What thirty years of enterprise sales taught me about coaching

By Joe Hliboki · June 2026

Last week of the quarter at Cisco. The forecast call is in an hour, the biggest deal on the board just went quiet, and a rep is standing at my desk telling me it's fine. It is not fine. We both know it's not fine.

I lived some version of that scene for thirty years, and here's what it taught me: pressure doesn't build character. Pressure reveals it. The habits people fall back on in that hour are the ones they actually have, not the ones they put on their resume. Everything I now do as a coach comes from watching what held up in that hour and what didn't.

Activity is not progress

Sales is full of busy people missing their number. Full calendars, full inboxes, empty quarters.

The discipline that saved me was learning to measure what closes instead of what's busy. Not how many calls went out, but how many moved a deal forward. Ask a struggling rep what they did this week and you get a long list. Ask what changed in the pipeline because of it and the room gets quiet.

Business owners run the same trap. Sixty-hour weeks can hide a stalled company for years. When I coach, the first question is never "what are you doing?" It's "what is that doing?"

The best reps wanted the hardest feedback

Every team I led had a top performer who came out of deal reviews asking for more. Tell me where I lost the room. Tell me what you would have done differently. It wasn't humility for show. It was greed, in the best sense, because they understood that soft feedback is a tax on their future earnings.

The strugglers wanted reassurance. The stars wanted the truth, ugly and specific.

Clients are exactly the same. The ones who grow fastest are the ones who ask me not to sand the edges off. So I don't. If a plan has a hole, hearing about it in a coaching session is a lot cheaper than finding it in the market.

A process you follow beats a strategy you admire

I've seen brilliant account strategies die in slide decks, and I've seen ordinary plans hit number after number because somebody ran them every single week. The difference was never intelligence. It was execution cadence.

A process only works if it survives contact with a bad month. That's why my engagements run on the FocalPoint structure: real commitments at the end of every session, checked at the start of the next one. Not because clients need homework. Because a strategy without a cadence is a wish.

People ask how a sales guy ends up coaching. Honestly, it's the same job in reverse. In sales, you spend your career getting the customer to yes. In coaching, you get the client to act. Same skills. Same honesty. Different side of the table.

If you've got a number to hit and a plan that keeps slipping, we should talk about this.
