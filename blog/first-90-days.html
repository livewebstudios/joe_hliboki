Your first 90 days: what actually matters

By Joe Hliboki · June 2026

I've watched a lot of first days. Thirty years of them: new reps, new managers, one memorable new VP who introduced himself to the whole floor with a slide deck about his own accomplishments. Most new hires make the same mistake in the first 90 days. They try to look smart. It's exactly backwards.

Nobody expects you to be smart yet. They expect you to learn fast. Those are different jobs, and the second one pays better.

Ask the questions everyone else is afraid to ask

The first 90 days is the only time "I don't know" is free. Use it.

Ask why the report is built that way. Ask what happened to the last person in your seat. Ask the question in the meeting that everyone senior is quietly wondering about but won't say out loud, because they think they're supposed to know already. In month two, that question reads as engagement. In year two, the same question reads as a gap. The window closes fast, so spend it hard while it's open.

Then write down the answers. More on that in a minute.

Find the person who's been there five years and buy them coffee

Every company has one. The person who knows why the org chart looks like that, and which customer is secretly a landmine. None of it is in the employee handbook. None of it ever will be, because institutional knowledge doesn't get written down. It gets carried around by people, and it beats the handbook every single time.

So find that person. Buy them coffee, ask how things really work around here, and then be quiet and listen. Twenty minutes with the right veteran will save you a full quarter of guessing wrong.

Deliver one small thing completely

New hires love to juggle. Big visible projects, lots of motion, nothing landing. Resist it.

Pick something small and finish it all the way. Tested, documented, handed off. Done means done. A finished small thing beats three impressive half-things every time, because the people watching you don't actually remember ambition. They remember whether the thing you said would happen, happened.

Reputation in a new job is built out of completed loops. Small ones count.

Take notes like it's your job, because it is

In your first month you'll hear a hundred names and twice as many acronyms. You will forget nearly all of it unless you write it down. So write it down. All of it.

Not because a notebook looks diligent in meetings. Because in week six, someone will mention the account everyone tiptoes around, and you'll be the new person who already knows the history. That isn't a memory trick. That's a habit, and habits are the whole game early. The notes you take in the first 90 days become the map you navigate with for the next two years.

Ninety days goes fast. You don't need to be impressive by day 91. You need to be trusted, and trust gets built from asked questions and closed loops. Momentum takes care of the rest.

I wrote a whole book about this stretch of a career and the years right after it. It's called Bursting the Bubble, and it's the rulebook school never handed you.
